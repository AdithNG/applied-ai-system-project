# Music Genre Reference

## pop
Pop music is characterized by catchy melodies, verse-chorus structure, and broad mainstream appeal. It typically features polished production, strong hooks, and lyrics about relationships and everyday emotions. Pop songs are built for accessibility — they aim to be immediately engaging and memorable. Artists like Taylor Swift, Dua Lipa, and The Weeknd exemplify modern pop: high production quality, energetic beats, and emotional relatability. Pop listeners generally expect a blend of upbeat energy, clear vocals, and danceable rhythms.

## lofi
Lo-fi (low fidelity) music deliberately embraces imperfections: subtle vinyl crackle, tape hiss, muffled beats, and warm analog textures. It originated from hip-hop but evolved into a genre associated with studying, relaxing, and focus. Tempos are slow to mid-range (60–90 BPM), and the overall feel is calm, introspective, and unobtrusive. Lo-fi is less about melody than about atmosphere — it creates a sonic environment that soothes without demanding attention. It pairs naturally with chill and focused moods.

## rock
Rock music is built around electric guitars, driving rhythms, and a sense of energy or rebellion. It spans a wide range of subgenres — from soft rock to heavy metal — but at its core emphasizes live instrumentation, dynamic contrasts, and emotional intensity. Classic rock bands like Led Zeppelin and modern acts like Foo Fighters demonstrate rock's range from melodic anthems to raw, aggressive sound. Rock listeners often seek power, authenticity, and cathartic release. High energy, distorted guitars, and strong drum patterns are hallmarks.

## classical
Classical music encompasses Western art music from roughly 1750 to 1820 (and is colloquially applied to broader orchestral traditions). It is characterized by complex harmonic structures, long-form compositions, and expressive dynamics ranging from delicate pianissimo to thundering fortissimo. Composers like Beethoven, Mozart, and Chopin shaped the tradition. Classical music rewards attentive listening — it builds tension and release across minutes or movements. It appeals to listeners who value emotional depth, technical mastery, and music free from lyrics.

## jazz
Jazz is an American musical tradition rooted in improvisation, syncopated rhythms, and complex chord progressions. Born from blues and ragtime, it spans bebop, smooth jazz, fusion, and more. A defining feature is the interplay between musicians — solos, call-and-response, and spontaneous variation. Artists like Miles Davis, John Coltrane, and Norah Jones represent jazz's range from abstract to accessible. Jazz rewards repeated listening; details and nuances reveal themselves over time. It suits listeners who appreciate sophistication, musical conversation, and emotional intimacy.

## hip-hop
Hip-hop centers on rhythmic vocal delivery (rap) over sample-based or synthesized beats. It emerged in New York in the 1970s and became one of the world's dominant genres. Lyricism — wordplay, storytelling, social commentary — is central to hip-hop identity. Producers like J Dilla and Kanye West transformed the sonic palette from breakbeats to orchestral samples. Modern hip-hop spans trap, boom bap, and melodic rap. Listeners often value authenticity, lyrical skill, and cultural relevance alongside bass-heavy, rhythmically complex production.

## electronic
Electronic music is produced primarily with synthesizers, drum machines, and digital audio workstations. It encompasses house, techno, ambient, EDM, and more. Its defining traits are programmed rhythms, synthesized textures, and a focus on sound design over traditional instrumentation. Electronic music ranges from introspective and atmospheric (ambient) to euphoric and high-energy (EDM). Artists like Daft Punk, Aphex Twin, and Calvin Harris demonstrate the genre's breadth. It suits listeners who value sonic experimentation, hypnotic grooves, and carefully crafted production.

## ambient
Ambient music prioritizes atmosphere, texture, and space over melody or rhythm. Pioneered by Brian Eno, it is designed to be heard as environment — it can be actively listened to or used as a sonic backdrop. Tempos are often absent or extremely slow; harmonic movement is glacial. Sounds are frequently derived from synthesizers, treated acoustic instruments, or field recordings. Ambient appeals to listeners who seek immersion, tranquility, and freedom from the demands of structured music.

## r&b
R&B (Rhythm and Blues) blends soulful vocals, smooth grooves, and emotional lyrical themes — typically love, sensuality, and personal relationships. Modern R&B draws from gospel, funk, and hip-hop, producing a warm, polished sound with expressive singing at the center. Artists like Beyoncé, Frank Ocean, and SZA define contemporary R&B. It appeals to listeners who value vocal performance, emotional authenticity, and music that feels both personal and danceable.

## indie
Indie (independent) music spans many styles but is united by a DIY ethos, artistic independence, and a tendency toward unconventional song structures or textures. Indie rock, indie pop, and indie folk all share a sense of intimacy and personal expression not always found in mainstream releases. Artists like Bon Iver, Vampire Weekend, and Phoebe Bridgers exemplify indie's range. Listeners often appreciate originality, emotional honesty, and a sense of discovery — finding something that feels personal rather than mass-produced.
